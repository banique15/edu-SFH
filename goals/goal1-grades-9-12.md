## Objectives

### Knowledge
- Identify and differentiate between the various dimensions of poverty, including material (economic hardship, lack of basic needs), social (exclusion, limited opportunities), and emotional (hopelessness, isolation) aspects. (NCCAS: Cr1.1.la, Cr2.1.la)
- Recognize the interconnectedness of these dimensions and how they can contribute to and perpetuate cycles of poverty at individual, community, and global levels. (NCCAS: Cn10.1.la, Cn11.1.la)
- Understand the global prevalence of poverty, examining its disproportionate impact on specific populations (e.g., women, children, marginalized ethnic groups) and regions, and explore the underlying systemic factors. (NCCAS: Cn10.1.la, Cn11.1.la)

### Discourse
- Analyze and interpret diverse perspectives on poverty across various art forms (music, visual art, creative writing, dance, drama), identifying the techniques used to convey meaning and evoke empathy. (NCCAS: Re7.1.la, Re8.1.la)
- Engage in respectful, informed discussions about the complex causes of poverty, its multifaceted effects, and potential multi-sectoral solutions, considering different viewpoints and lived experiences. (NCCAS: Re9.1.la, Cn11.1.la)
- Express their own nuanced thoughts, feelings, and developing ideas about poverty through thoughtful and purposeful creative expression in various artistic mediums. (NCCAS: Cr3.1.la)

### Attitudes
- Develop a deepened sense of empathy and compassion for individuals and communities experiencing poverty, moving beyond stereotypes to understand the human impact. (NCCAS: Re8.1.la, Cn10.1.la)
- Challenge preconceived stereotypes, assumptions, and biases about poverty and the diverse individuals and communities affected by it, fostering a more nuanced and informed perspective. (NCCAS: Re9.1.la, Cn10.1.la)
- Cultivate a strong sense of social responsibility and ethical awareness, fostering a personal desire to contribute to positive social change and advocate for a more equitable world. (NCCAS: Cn11.1.la)

### Capacity
- Develop advanced critical thinking and analytical skills to investigate the root causes of poverty (e.g., systemic inequality, historical injustices, lack of access to resources) and evaluate the effectiveness and ethical implications of potential solutions. (NCCAS: Re9.1.la, Cn10.1.la)
- Enhance sophisticated communication, collaboration, and creative problem-solving skills through in-depth group activities, artistic collaborations, and the development of impactful creative projects. (NCCAS: Cr2.1.la, Pr5.1.la)
- Gain a comprehensive understanding of the unique and powerful role of arts and culture in raising critical awareness, fostering empathy, challenging dominant narratives, and inspiring meaningful action on complex social issues like poverty. (NCCAS: Cn11.1.la)

### Action
- Identify and critically evaluate age-appropriate and impactful actions that can be taken to address poverty at local, national, and global levels, considering the complexities of systemic change. (NCCAS: Pr6.1.la, Cn11.1.la)
- Develop, collaboratively plan, and implement thoughtful and ethical action plans or artistic interventions aimed at making a positive impact on the issue of poverty within their communities or beyond. (NCCAS: Cr3.1.la, Pr6.1.la)
- Reflect critically on their evolving role as engaged global citizens and articulate a personal commitment to advocating for social justice and working towards a more equitable and poverty-free world. (NCCAS: Re9.1.la, Cn11.1.la)

## Action Planning

Empowering youth to become active agents of change in the pursuit of ending poverty in all its forms everywhere. Even small, consistent actions can contribute to a larger global impact.

### Small, Everyday Actions to Inspire Change
- Embrace Mindful Consumption: Think about where your clothes and products come from. Supporting ethical brands that pay fair wages can indirectly help combat poverty by promoting equitable labor practices.
- Reduce Food Waste: Be conscious of the food you buy and consume. Reducing food waste can conserve resources and, on a larger scale, impact food security for those in need.
- Share Your Skills: If you have a skill (e.g., tutoring, gardening, crafting), consider sharing it with someone in your community who could benefit, fostering a sense of mutual support.
- Be Resourceful: Find creative ways to reuse, repurpose, or repair items instead of immediately buying new ones. This can help reduce consumption and promote sustainability.
- Support Local Businesses: When possible, choose to buy from local businesses. This helps stimulate the local economy and can provide stable employment opportunities in your community.
- Educate Yourself and Others: Stay informed about the different dimensions of poverty (material, social, emotional) and challenge stereotypes. Share accurate information with friends and family to foster greater understanding.
- Practice Empathy and Respect: Treat everyone with dignity and respect, regardless of their socioeconomic status. Challenge your own biases and assumptions about individuals experiencing poverty.
- Conserve Resources: Be mindful of your use of water, electricity, and other resources. Sustainable practices contribute to a healthier planet, which is crucial for vulnerable populations often disproportionately affected by environmental shocks.
- Advocate for Fair Practices: If you observe unfair labor practices or economic injustices, find safe and appropriate ways to voice your concerns to relevant authorities or organizations.
- Listen to Lived Experiences: Seek out and listen to stories from individuals who have experiences poverty. This can deepen your understanding and empathy.

### Holistic Actions

#### Awareness and Advocacy (Using Your Voice)
- Use your artistic skills (music, visual art, creative writing, dance, drama) to develop pieces that shed light on the realities of poverty, challenge stereotypes, and inspire action. Share these creations in school, online, or through community exhibits.
- Lead short presentations or discussions in class, during club meetings, or for community groups to raise awareness about Global Goal 1 and its importance. Focus on local and global impacts of poverty.
- Research and engage with organizations working on poverty alleviation. Participate in letter-writing campaigns or petition drives advocating for policies that promote social protection, access to resources, and economic opportunities for all.

#### Local Engagement and Support (Acting in Your Community)
- Identify and volunteer your time with local food banks, shelters, community centers, or organizations that provide support services to individuals and families experiencing poverty. This directly contributes to addressing immediate needs.
- Lead collection drives within your school or community for essential items such as non-perishable food, clothing, hygiene products, or school supplies, to be distributed to those in need.
- Research and support local initiatives that aim to provide skills training, job placement, or microfinance opportunities to individuals seeking to overcome poverty.
- Get involved in local projects that aim to build community resilience against shocks and disasters, such as community gardens, emergency preparedness programs, or neighborhood clean-up efforts.

#### Global Citizenship and Learning (Expanding Your Understanding)
- Research the interconnectedness of global issues and how poverty in one region can impact others. Investigate how historical injustices or global economic policies contribute to poverty worldwide.
- Stay informed about global poverty trends, report from international organizations (like the UN or World Bank), and the progress towards Global Goal 1. Understand the statistics and indicators related to poverty reductions.
- Understand the concept of fair trade and actively seek out fair trade certified products. This ensures that producers in developing countries receive fair compensation for their work, supporting sustainable livelihoods and economic empowerment.
- Broaden your understanding of different cultures and perspectives, especially those in regions heavily impacted by poverty. This can foster greater empathy and challenge ethnocentric biases.
